The Ideal
Gas Equation
pV = nRT
pV = NkT
The Ideal Gas equation
relates pressure, volume,
amount of substance and
temperature for an ideal gas
p = pressure (Pa)
V = volume (m3)
n = number of moles
R = ideal gas const.
N = number of particles
k = Boltzmann const.
T = temperature
What is Temperature?
Temperature is the measure
of the average kinetic energy
of particles in a substance.
It also determines the
direction of thermal
energy transfer.
Thermal energy will move
from areas of high temperature
to areas of lower temperature.
Temperature Scales
There are 3 main scales
and units for temperature
Celcius
Celcius is the most widely
used temperature scale and
is part of the standard system
of units in most metric countries.

It is defined by the boiling point
of water (100°C) and it's melting
point (0°C)
Kelvin
Fahrenheit
The Kelvin scale is used
by the SI system of units,
and while it uses the same
increments as celcius (an
increase of 1°C = an increase
of 1K), 0 Kelvin is defined as
absolute zero, so 0K = 273°C.

This makes it an absolute scale,
like all other SI units
Fahrenheit is the imperial temperature
scale, and as such is mainly used in
North America.

It defines 0°F as the freezing point of
ammonium chloride brine, and 96°F
as the temperature of the human body.
The Kinetic Model
The Kinetic Model Decribes the
makeup of substances based on
atoms/molecules, and the different
ways they are arranged based on
their state.
Gas
Liquid
Solid
In a liquid, while the particles are
closely packed they are free to
move over each other within the
substance. This gives liquids their
viscosity, and creates surface tension
allowing them to form droplets among
other things.
In a solid, particles are closely packed
into a fixed lattice-like structure. While
this structure can depend on the substance,
with some substances forming different
lattices the concept is largely the same. The
particles also vibrate in place, giving the solid
its temperature
In a gas, particles are far apart
and have almost to intermolecular
forces, which also causes them to
be able to move around at high speed,
creating gas pressure. The spacing of
the particles also gives gases their
very low density and high volume.
Brownian Motion
Brownian Motion is the name given to the
phonomenon in which something suspended
in a fluid (liquid or gas) moves around seemingly
randomly, constantly changing direction. This
mainly occurs to very small, light particles eg pollen
or smoke particles.
This is caused by the constant collisions
with the particles of the fluid which are
constantly moving around in all directions.
The Kinetic Model also describes
why temperature cannot go below
0 Kelvin, or Absolute Zero.

At Absolute Zero, a substance has
no Kinetic Energy and as such the
pareticles will stop moving
completely, and the temperature
cannot be lowered any further.

Specific Latent Heat
The specific latent heat of a
substance is the energy
required per kilogram of the
substance to change its state
without any change in
temperature.
Specific Latent Heat of Fusion
refers to the energy required
for a change from solid to liquid.
Specific Latent Heat of Vaporisation
refers to the energy required
for a change from solid to liquid.

During a change of state, any energy
transferred to a substance is used by
the state change - hence the definition
states "without any change in

An ideal gas is a way of describing
the behavior of a gas using a set of
laws and equations.
An Ideal Gas has no intermolecular forces,
and all collisions between molecules and
with surfaces are perfectly elastic.
This also means an ideal gas'
internal energy is made entirely
of its kinetic energy.
According to the ideal gas equation, as
long as the amount of substance and
temperature are constant, the pressure
multiplied by the volume is constant.
This means as volume
increases, pressure
decreases and vice versa.
However, as temperature or the amount
of substance increase, pressure and/or
volume must also increase.
These values are all connected
by a constant - usually the Ideal
Gas Constant, which is around 8.31.
When the number of particles
is used in place of the number of
moles, the Boltmann Constant
is used instead.
